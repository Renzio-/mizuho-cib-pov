## 3. Regulatory Context — MAS & Singapore CIB

### Key MAS Regulations Affecting CIB Operations

| Regulation | Relevance to Mizuho CIB |
|---|---|
| **MAS Notice 637 (Risk-Based Capital)** | Basel III/IV capital adequacy requirements. Mizuho Singapore must maintain prescribed CAR ratios. Drives need for precise portfolio-level visibility. |
| **MAS Technology Risk Management (TRM) Guidelines** | Mandates robust tech risk governance for all financial institutions. Updated 2024 — expanded requirements for AI/ML model governance, cloud computing, and data management. |
| **MAS FEAT Principles** | Fairness, Ethics, Accountability, Transparency for AI/ML in financial services. Not legally binding but strongly supervisory-expected. |
| **MAS Notice 610 / AML/CFT** | Anti-money laundering and countering financing of terrorism. Critical for CIB cross-border transactions. Enhanced CDD requirements for complex corporate structures. |
| **MAS Environmental Risk Management Guidelines** | ESG disclosure and climate risk assessment requirements for banks. Relevant to Mizuho's energy/resources CIB portfolio. |
| **MAS Business Conduct Regulations** | Fair dealing, conflict of interest management — relevant to advisory and S&T operations. |

### Recent Regulatory Developments (2024-2025)

1. **MAS TRM Guidelines update (Jan 2025)** — Expanded Section 5 on technology risk governance to include explicit requirements for AI model risk management, algorithmic audits, and explainability. Financial institutions using AI for credit decisions or client-facing recommendations must demonstrate model validation.

2. **MAS GenAI sandbox / Veritas initiative** — MAS has been running industry consortiums (Project Veritas) to develop responsible AI assessment methodologies. Participating banks expected to adopt these frameworks.

3. **Cross-border data transfer tightening** — PDPA amendments and MAS guidelines increasingly require demonstrable controls over cross-border data flows — critical for a Japanese bank operating across APAC with centralized systems in Tokyo.

4. **Climate risk stress testing** — MAS industry-wide climate stress test (2024-2025) requires banks to model portfolio impact under various climate scenarios. CIB lending books (especially energy, infrastructure) heavily affected.

5. **Enhanced individual accountability** — MAS Guidelines on Individual Accountability and Conduct (IAC) — senior managers must demonstrate oversight of business activities, including technology-related decisions.

> **Source note:** MAS TRM Guidelines are publicly available on mas.gov.sg. FEAT principles published 2022, with ongoing industry adoption. Climate stress test mandated in 2024. Cross-border data requirements reflect broader ASEAN regulatory trend. **Verify any 2026 regulatory changes via MAS website.**

## 5. "Why Now" Signals — Urgency Drivers for Mizuho CIB AI Adoption

### Signal 1: MUFG's Salesforce Deployment Creates Immediate Competitive Gap

**The pressure:** MUFG — Mizuho's closest peer and direct competitor for Japanese corporate cross-border mandates — has already gone live with Salesforce across CIB. Their RMs have unified client views, AI-assisted pipeline management, and cross-geography collaboration tools. Every quarter Mizuho delays, the service gap widens for shared clients.

**The risk:** Japanese corporates expanding in APAC (Toyota, Sony, SoftBank, etc.) work with all three mega-banks. If MUFG's RM delivers faster, more informed coverage while Mizuho's RM is manually assembling client data across systems, the mandates shift.

**Connects to Mizuho's stated strategy:** "Expanding non-interest income and revising portfolio by extending the CIB model to Asia-Pacific and EMEA" — you cannot extend a model that doesn't have the platform infrastructure to scale.

---

### Signal 2: MAS Regulatory Pressure Demands Auditable, Governed AI & Client Data Controls

**The pressure:** MAS TRM Guidelines (2025 update) now explicitly require AI model governance, explainability, and auditable decision trails. Mizuho Singapore cannot simply bolt AI onto fragmented systems — they need a governed platform that provides:
- Audit trails for client interactions and recommendations
- Explainable AI outputs (FEAT principles)
- Data lineage for cross-border information flows
- Individual accountability traceability (IAC guidelines)

**The risk:** Operating without proper governance infrastructure means either: (a) avoiding AI adoption entirely (falling behind competitors), or (b) deploying AI without proper controls (regulatory risk). Neither is acceptable.

**Connects to Mizuho's stated strategy:** "Enhance the framework of internal controls aligned with increasingly demanding supervisory perspectives" — Salesforce provides the auditable, governed platform to deploy AI responsibly under MAS oversight.

---

### Signal 3: Asia-Pacific CIB Expansion Requires Scalable Relationship Intelligence Now

**The pressure:** Mizuho's mid-term plan explicitly targets non-interest income growth by extending CIB into APAC. Singapore is the regional hub. But scaling CIB coverage across ASEAN requires:
- Unified visibility into client relationships across Tokyo, Singapore, HK, Sydney
- AI-powered identification of cross-sell opportunities (derivatives for existing lending clients)
- Rapid onboarding of new RM capacity with institutional knowledge embedded in the platform
- Real-time collaboration between product specialists (S&T, DCM, project finance) and coverage bankers

**The risk:** Attempting to scale CIB coverage with fragmented, legacy CRM (or no CRM) means each new RM starts from scratch, cross-border collaboration is manual, and revenue opportunities slip through the cracks. The S&T derivatives growth strategy specifically requires tight coordination between trading desks and coverage — impossible without a shared platform.

**Connects to Mizuho's stated strategy:** "S&T strategy is to grow derivatives in Asia ex-Japan" — derivatives cross-sell requires relationship intelligence that connects lending relationships to trading opportunities. That's a CRM + AI problem.
